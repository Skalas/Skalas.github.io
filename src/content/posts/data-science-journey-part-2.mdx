## What actually changed

{/* TODO(miguel): the most personal section. Pick the 2–3 things that feel genuinely different day-to-day compared to 2018 data science. Some hooks you can use or discard: */}

- **The tools got weird.** I spend more time writing prompts and evals than writing SQL. The evals are harder than the prompts.
- **The ceiling moved.** Problems I used to triage as "not worth building a model for" are now a forty-five-minute experiment.
- **The ground moved.** Things I spent years getting good at (feature engineering, classical ML hyperparameter tuning, hand-tuned recsys) matter less. Not zero, but less.

## What didn't change

{/* TODO(miguel): this is the throughline to Part 1. Here's what I'd argue carries over — edit as needed: */}

Most of the job is still plumbing. The model is still ten percent. The rest is still:

- Getting the data into shape.
- Getting a human to act on the output.
- Making the thing run tomorrow morning without you babysitting it.

LLMs did not abolish any of that. If anything they moved it around. Now instead of a malformed CSV you get a confidently hallucinated JSON.

## What I'd tell my 2024 self

{/* TODO(miguel): mirror the structure of Part 1's closing. Two short pieces of advice. Examples to riff on: */}

**Evaluation is the job now.** The model is a commodity; the eval suite is the product. If you are not measuring your system more carefully than you built it, you are guessing.

**Speed of iteration beats cleverness of architecture.** The teams winning right now are the ones who can run a new experiment in an hour, not the ones who designed the fanciest system.
